Logical Fallacies: What They Are, How to Spot Them, and Why They're Wrecking Your Decisions
Most bad arguments don't announce themselves. They arrive dressed in data, expert quotes, and reasonable-sounding logic. The flaw is in the structure, not the surface.
That is what makes logical fallacies so persistent. You can be analytical, rigorous, even genuinely data-driven, and still fall into them. A hasty generalization feels like a reasonable conclusion. Post hoc reasoning looks like cause and effect. A false dilemma sounds like clear thinking.
If you do any kind of optimization, research, or decision-making at work, these errors are showing up in your meetings. Probably weekly. This guide will help you name them, recognize them, and stop making them.

What is a logical fallacy?
A logical fallacy is an error in reasoning that makes an argument invalid, even when the conclusion might appear correct.
This is worth sitting with. A fallacy is not the same as a false statement. An argument can contain true premises and still be fallacious if the conclusion does not follow from those premises. Equally, a fallacious argument can accidentally arrive at a true conclusion. The problem is with the reasoning, not necessarily the result.
Aristotle was the first to systematically catalog fallacies in Sophistical Refutations (around 350 BCE), identifying 13 types of flawed arguments used by sophists to win debates through misdirection. Indian logicians developed similar frameworks independently in the Nyaya Sutras. Richard Whately's 1826 Elements of Logic formalized the modern classification system that most researchers still use.
In marketing, testing, and business decision-making, fallacies appear constantly: in how teams interpret experiment results, in how they justify strategy changes, in how they evaluate what competitors are doing. Learning to name them is the first step to catching them.

Formal vs. informal fallacies: the core distinction
All logical fallacies fall into one of two categories.
Formal fallacies
A formal fallacy is an error in the logical structure of an argument. You can identify it by examining the form alone, without knowing anything about the subject matter.
Three common formal fallacies worth knowing:
Affirming the consequent: "If it rained, the ground is wet. The ground is wet. Therefore it rained." The ground could be wet for other reasons. The form is invalid regardless of content.
Denying the antecedent: "If you study, you'll pass. You didn't study. Therefore you won't pass." There are other ways to pass. Again, invalid form.
Undistributed middle: "All cats are mammals. All dogs are mammals. Therefore all cats are dogs." The shared property does not establish a connection between the two groups.
Informal fallaciesInformal fallacies are errors in content, context, or delivery, not logical structure. These 
are far more common in everyday life. Their validity depends on the subject matter, so you have to look at content, not just form.
They break down further into three types:
Fallacies of relevance: The premise has nothing to do with the conclusion (ad hominem, red herring, appeal to authority)
Fallacies of ambiguity: The argument exploits unclear or shifting language (equivocation)
Fallacies of presumption: The argument assumes what it needs to prove (circular reasoning, false dilemma, hasty generalization)
The rest of this article focuses on informal fallacies. These are the ones appearing in your strategy meetings.

Common logical fallacies: definitions and examples
1. Hasty generalization
A hasty generalization is an informal fallacy where you draw a broad conclusion from a sample that is too small, unrepresentative, or selected without rigor. You make a hasty conclusion without considering all variables, usually driven by a small sample size and impatience.
Wikipedia gives a clear example: "If a person travels through a town for the first time and sees 10 people, all of them children, they may erroneously conclude that there are no adult residents in the town."
If you have ever read anything about A/B testing, this already sounds familiar: do not stop tests early. It is one of the most expensive errors in experimentation.
You implement a test. After 48 hours, variation B is losing badly. It seems obvious. But if you keep the test running, the trends often even out and variation B may end up winning. Stopping early based on a few data points is a textbook hasty generalization.
The same error appears in qualitative research. You collect 1,000 survey responses. You read the first 20 and notice 14 mention shipping concerns. You prioritize shipping as a critical issue without reading the remaining 980, which may render shipping a minor problem. You have generalized from a highly unrepresentative sample.
Bottom line: Small datasets are deceptive. Extreme trends at the beginning tend to normalize over time. Define your required sample size before you start, then hold to it. As Peep Laja has said, "You can't test faster just because you or your boss or your VCs want to move faster. That's not how math works."
Related fallacy: Slothful induction. The opposite error: refusing to accept a well-supported conclusion despite strong evidence. If a test reached significance and the data is clean, take the result.

2. Appeal to authority
An appeal to authority is when, in the face of conflicting evidence, you invoke the opinion of an expert to settle an argument. The problem is not referencing experts. The problem is treating expert opinion as a replacement for evidence rather than one form of it.
Carl Sagan put it clearly: "One of the great commandments of science is, 'Mistrust arguments from authority.' Too many such arguments have proved too painfully wrong. Authorities must prove their contentions like everybody else."
In optimization, this fallacy appears in two ways. First: "Neil Patel said X matters most, so we should test X." Your website is not Neil Patel's. His audience, product, and context may be entirely different from yours. His heuristics are a starting point for forming hypotheses, not a substitute for testing them.
Second: "Amazon does it this way." Amazon operates at a scale, with a user base, and under competitive pressures that almost certainly do not apply to your product. Copying their decisions without understanding the evidence behind them is an appeal to authority.
As one Wordstream author noted: "Just because eBay is a big company with a big marketing budget doesn't mean that whoever's in charge of their PPC knows what they're doing. Big companies, whole empires even, fail all the time. Authority doesn't equal competence."
Bottom line: Experts are useful. Their heuristics tend to be more reliable than guesses. But "an expert said so" is not a valid argument on its own when making decisions. It limits the scope of your thinking and anchors you to the status quo. Treat expert opinion as a hypothesis to test, not a conclusion to accept.

3. Appeal to tradition
The appeal to tradition, also known as argumentum ad antiquitatem, is a fallacy where an idea is assumed to be correct because it has always been done that way. If you work in any organization that has been around for more than two years, you have heard this argument.
Many company cultures are mired in allegiance to tradition and genuinely resistant to change, which is the opposite of a culture of experimentation. The argument "we've always done it this way" is not evidence that the current approach is optimal. It is evidence that the current approach survived long enough to become default.
As Logically Fallacious puts it: "If it weren't for the creativity of our ancestors, we would have no traditions. Be creative and start your own traditions that somehow make the world a better place." The point is not to discard everything old. The point is that age alone does not validate a practice.
Bottom line: A company's tradition of success is not caused by the tradition itself. "Because it's always been done this way" is not a valid argument for continuing. Build a culture that values evidence over precedent.
Related fallacy: Appeal to novelty. The opposite error. Assuming something is better simply because it is new. In optimization, this shows up as spaghetti testing: running experiments to introduce novelty rather than because evidence suggests a change will improve outcomes. Neither age nor newness is evidence of value.

4. Post hoc ergo propter hoc
Post hoc ergo propter hoc translates to "after this, therefore because of this." It is the error of establishing causation where there is only correlation. If one event follows another, many people intuitively conclude the first caused the second.
A passage from Voltaire's Candide illustrates this perfectly:
"It is demonstrable that things cannot be otherwise than as they are; for as all things have been created for some end, they must necessarily be created for the best end. Observe, for instance, the nose is formed for spectacles, therefore we wear spectacles."
In marketing this fallacy is everywhere. You implement a new value proposition on May 1st. Conversions drop 15% by June 1st. You conclude the value proposition caused the drop. But it could be seasonality, a competitor's action, a PR story, a pricing change, or a dozen other factors. Without a controlled experiment, you cannot establish causation.
Steven Levitt of Freakonomics described a perfect real-world example. A client had been running newspaper ads before major holidays and crediting them with subsequent revenue spikes. When Levitt proposed a controlled experiment, placing ads in some locations and not others, the finding was unambiguous: the ads made no measurable difference at all. The revenue spikes would have happened regardless.
Ben Yoskovitz, co-author of Lean Analytics, explains the solution: "You prove causality by finding a correlation, then running experiments where you control the other variables and measure the difference. It's hard to do, but causality is really an analytics superpower. It gives you the power to hack the future."
Bottom line: Correlation does not imply causation. Visitors who do X converting Y times better does not mean X causes the improvement. The only reliable cure is to run controlled experiments. And even then, be careful about confounding variables, especially if you run a seasonal business.

5. False dilemma
A false dilemma, also known as a false dichotomy, presents only two options when more exist. It is frequently used as a rhetorical device to force a choice between an acceptable position and an unacceptable one, eliminating the actual range of alternatives.
Politically, this appears as "you're either with us or against us," collapsing a complex landscape of positions into a binary that serves the speaker.
In testing, the equivalent is congruence bias: the tendency to test only the most obvious version of a hypothesis and treat a negative result as disconfirmation of the entire idea. Andrew Anderson, formerly Head of Optimization at Recovery Brands, described it this way:
"You create a false choice such as one banner versus another or one headline versus another, and then test to see which is better. In reality, there are hundreds of different ways that you can go, but we become blinded by our myopic view of an answer. It is the job of the researcher or tester to compare all valid alternative hypotheses, yet we get stuck on the things we want to see win."
Here is a practical example. Research shows users are uncomfortable entering credit card information on your checkout page. So you test security logo vs. no security logo. The result shows no difference. You conclude the hypothesis was wrong.
But the hypothesis was not that a security logo would solve the problem. The hypothesis was that users felt unsafe during checkout. There are dozens of ways to address that concern. You tested one execution and dismissed the idea. That is a false dilemma.
Bottom line: A failed test is a failed execution, not a failed hypothesis. Ask yourself: how many different ways could this idea be expressed and tested? Iterative testing is the answer to congruence bias. As Anderson advises: "Always make sure you avoid only testing what you think will win, and ensure that you are designing tests around what is feasible, not just the most popular opinion about how to solve the current problem."

6. Narrative fallacy
The narrative fallacy, named and popularized by Nassim Taleb in The Black Swan, is the tendency to construct a coherent causal story to explain a sequence of events, even when the events may be unrelated or the causation is unknown. The human mind is pattern-seeking by design. We are uncomfortable with randomness and tend to assign meaning to sequences whether or not meaning exists.
You cannot read a Malcolm Gladwell book without encountering this. In Outliers, Gladwell connects rice farming to superior math performance in Asian cultures through a chain of narrative logic that feels compelling and is almost impossible to verify causally. The story is satisfying. That is the problem.
In optimization, Andrew Anderson used a revealing exercise with new consultants: take a test you have not seen the results of, and explain why every variant is clearly the best option and why it will obviously win. Initially consultants resist. By the fourth or fifth test, they can do it effortlessly, constructing a persuasive narrative for any possible outcome.
As Anderson explains: "One of the greatest mistakes people make is confusing the 'why' they generate in their head with the actual experience they test. You can arrive at an experience in any number of ways, and the story you tell yourself is just the mental model you used to create it. The experience is independent of that model."
Bottom line: Stop trying to assign a definitive why to your data. If variant B won, "it won" is a fact. "It won because red conveys urgency and our users need urgency to convert" is a story you made up after the fact. Test the why separately. Do not let a narrative govern the direction of the next experiment.

7. Ad hominem
Ad hominem means "to the person." Instead of addressing an argument, you attack the person making it.
Direct: "You can't trust his analysis, he doesn't have a statistics background." This may be relevant context, but it does not make the analysis wrong. The data either supports the conclusion or it does not.
Circumstantial: "Of course she supports this strategy, it benefits her team." A conflict of interest is worth disclosing. It does not, by itself, invalidate the argument.
Tu quoque (you too): "You can't criticize my methodology, yours isn't perfect either." This deflects without responding. Even if true, it is not a counterargument.
The test for ad hominem is simple: could the argument be made by a different person and still be valid? If yes, the attack on the person is irrelevant to the argument's strength.

8. Straw man
A straw man argument misrepresents an opponent's position to make it easier to attack. You argue against a distorted, weaker version of what was actually said.
A team proposes moving from weekly to monthly reporting. A critic responds: "So you want to fly blind for 30 days with no visibility into performance?" The original proposal said nothing about removing monitoring. The critic built a straw version of it and knocked that down instead.
The tell: the person being responded to feels their position has been distorted. When you suspect a straw man, restate the original argument clearly. "That is not what I said. My argument was X. Do you have a response to that?"

9. Slippery slope
A slippery slope argument claims that a small first step will inevitably lead to extreme consequences, without evidence that the intermediate steps are likely.
"If we allow employees to work from home on Fridays, soon no one will come in and team culture will collapse entirely." Each step in that chain requires evidence. The catastrophic conclusion does not follow automatically from the initial change.
A slippery slope argument is not always a fallacy. If there is genuine evidence that the intermediate steps are likely, the argument can be valid. The fallacy is asserting the chain without evidence, not identifying a causal sequence.

10. Appeal to emotion
An appeal to emotion uses emotional manipulation to persuade in place of evidence or reasoning. Fear, guilt, flattery, and pride are the most common levers.
This matters in two different contexts. In persuasion and copywriting, emotional resonance is legitimate and often necessary. In argumentation and decision-making, emotion is not a substitute for evidence. When the only basis for a claim is how it makes someone feel, the claim has not been supported.
The distinction is relevant when evaluating messaging tests. A variant that "feels right" or generates emotional enthusiasm in a review session is not the same as a variant that converts better in a controlled experiment.

11. Bandwagon fallacy
The bandwagon fallacy argues that something is correct or desirable because many people believe or do it. "80% of companies are now using this framework" tells you about adoption rates. It tells you nothing about whether the framework is effective.
Social proof is a legitimate psychological lever in persuasion. The bandwagon fallacy is the logical error of treating popularity as evidence of quality. Something can be universally adopted and still be wrong. For most of recorded history, the consensus view was that the sun orbited the earth.

12. Confirmation bias
Confirmation bias is the tendency to seek, interpret, and remember information that confirms existing beliefs while discounting information that contradicts them. It is less a rhetorical fallacy than a cognitive one, but it is among the most consequential errors in reasoning for anyone running experiments.
You hypothesize that adding a video to the landing page will increase conversion. You run a test. Results are inconclusive. You find methodological reasons to discount the result, run again, and continue until you have something you can accept. This is confirmation bias degrading your experimental process from the inside.
The structural fix is pre-registration: commit to your hypothesis, your success metrics, and your decision criteria before the experiment runs. Assigning someone to actively steelman the opposing hypothesis also helps. These do not eliminate the bias. They reduce its influence.

13. Sunk cost fallacy
The sunk cost fallacy is continuing to invest in something because of past investment, even when the rational decision is to stop.
A team spends 18 months building a feature. User adoption data is poor. Leadership continues investing because "we've come too far to quit." The 18 months are gone regardless of what happens next. The only rational question is whether future investment will generate sufficient return.
In testing: running an experiment longer than the data justifies because setting it up took significant effort is a version of this fallacy. Previous effort is not a logical reason to continue.

14. False cause
False cause is incorrectly identifying what caused something, typically by selecting the most convenient explanation while ignoring confounding variables.
Ice cream sales and drowning rates are positively correlated. Ice cream does not cause drowning. Both increase in summer. The season is the confounding variable neither metric accounts for. This is an obvious example because it involves ice cream. In business data, confounding variables are usually less obvious and more dangerous.
Before claiming causation, ask: is there a plausible mechanism directly linking these two things? Are there confounding variables that could explain both? Has a controlled experiment ruled out the alternatives?

15. Circular reasoning
Circular reasoning uses the conclusion as a premise in the argument intended to support it.
"This product is the market leader because more people buy it than any competitor. More people buy it because it's the market leader." The two claims explain each other. Neither is independently supported.
In research and strategy, circular reasoning often appears when people define their terms in ways that presuppose their conclusions. The argument looks valid on the surface. Underneath, nothing is actually being supported.

16. Appeal to ignorance
An appeal to ignorance claims that something is true because it has not been proven false, or false because it has not been proven true.
"No study has proven this supplement doesn't work, so it must work." The absence of disconfirming evidence is not confirmation. This is also called arguing from silence.
The burden of proof lies with the person making a positive claim. "You cannot disprove my claim" is not evidence that the claim is true.

17. Equivocation
Equivocation uses a word with multiple meanings in different ways within the same argument, creating a misleading conclusion.
In business, equivocation often appears in performance reporting. Terms like "engagement," "reach," "conversion," and "qualified lead" can mean very different things to different people. When definitions shift mid-argument without acknowledgment, the conclusion does not follow from the premises.
Before accepting any data-based argument, check: does everyone in this conversation mean the same thing by the key terms?

18. Red herring
A red herring introduces an irrelevant topic to distract from the original issue.
A team member challenges the statistical validity of an experiment. Instead of addressing the methodology, the project lead responds by describing how much effort went into the project and how strategically important the initiative is. Neither the effort nor the importance addresses the methodological concern. The original question has been buried.
Red herrings work because the new topic sounds related. The test: has the original question actually been answered?

19. No true Scotsman
The no true Scotsman fallacy modifies a universal claim to exclude counterexamples, making the original claim unfalsifiable.
"No real growth marketer would make that mistake." When presented with a growth marketer who made the mistake: "Well, she's not a real growth marketer then."
The category has been redefined to exclude any possible counterexample. The claim cannot be disproven because the definition shifts whenever a counterexample appears. This is sometimes called moving the goalposts.

20. Genetic fallacy
The genetic fallacy judges an argument based on its origin rather than its merit.
"That research was funded by a company with a commercial interest in the result, so the findings are wrong." The funding source is relevant context for evaluating credibility. It does not automatically invalidate the findings. The data has to be evaluated on its own terms.
This is related to but distinct from ad hominem. Ad hominem attacks the person making an argument. The genetic fallacy attacks where the argument came from.

How to spot logical fallacies in real arguments
Spotting fallacies in your own reasoning is harder than spotting them in other people. The following process helps.
Separate the structure from the delivery. Take any argument and strip away the confidence, the emotion, and the authority of the person making it. Write out: what is the claim? What are the premises offered in support? Does the conclusion actually follow from those premises? An argument can feel compelling because of strong emotion or familiar examples. Remove those and see if the structure holds.
Ask whether the argument responds to what was actually said. Straw man arguments, red herrings, and ad hominem attacks all share one feature: they respond to something other than the original claim. When an argument seems to change the subject, go back to the original question and ask whether it was answered.
Check for missing alternatives. False dilemmas, slippery slopes, and hasty generalizations all depend on artificially narrowing the range of possibilities. Ask: are these really the only two options? Is every step in this chain actually likely? Is this sample representative of all relevant cases?
Ask what evidence supports the causal claim. Post hoc reasoning, false cause, and narrative fallacy all assert causation without sufficient evidence. Is there a controlled experiment behind this claim? What alternative explanations have been ruled out? Is correlation being treated as proof of causation?
Watch for shifting definitions. When a key term appears more than once in an argument, check whether it means the same thing each time. If the definition shifts, the conclusion may not follow.

How to build stronger arguments
Recognizing fallacies in other people is useful. Avoiding them in your own reasoning is more useful still.
State your premises explicitly. Most fallacious arguments hide weak premises. Forcing yourself to articulate every assumption you are making exposes where the reasoning is thin.
Steelman the opposing view. Before arguing against a position, construct the strongest possible version of it. If you cannot explain why a reasonable person might hold the opposing view, you do not understand the argument well enough to refute it.
Separate fact from interpretation. "Variant B increased conversion by 12%" is a fact. "Variant B increased conversion because users responded to the social proof element" is an interpretation. Keep these distinct in your communications and in your own thinking.
Seek disconfirming evidence. Confirmation bias is the default mode of human cognition. Actively looking for evidence that contradicts your hypothesis is not pessimism. It is rigor. A hypothesis you have tried hard to disprove and failed is more reliable than one you have only tried to confirm.
Apply the burden of proof consistently. The person making a claim bears the burden of supporting it. This applies to your own claims as much as to anyone else's. "We should do X" requires a reason. "No one has proven we shouldn't" is not a reason.

Conclusion
The most expensive limitations of a testing program rarely have anything to do with data. They center on human reasoning errors that are invisible precisely because they feel like sound thinking.
The six fallacies covered in the original CXL article remain the most dangerous to experimentation programs: hasty generalization, appeal to authority, appeal to tradition, post hoc reasoning, false dilemma, and narrative fallacy. These are the errors that cause teams to stop tests early, copy competitors without evidence, abandon good hypotheses after a single failed execution, and construct explanations that feel certain but explain nothing.
The additional 14 fallacies in this guide appear in strategy, communication, research interpretation, and the day-to-day arguments that shape how teams operate.
Learning to name them is step one. The actual value comes from applying that recognition in the moment: in a meeting where a decision is being made on insufficient evidence, in a report where correlation is being dressed up as causation, in a test that is about to be stopped too early.

Bad decisions rarely come from bad data. They come from flawed reasoning around good data that seemed perfectly logical at the time.
Logical fallacies are structural errors in reasoning—they mimic the structure of sound reasoning without the substance. The conclusion sounds right. The logic tracks, mostly. But somewhere along the way, a step got skipped, a cause got assumed, or two options got treated as the only options. You've done it. Everyone in your last strategy meeting has done it.
The hard part isn't understanding what logical fallacies are. Recognizing them when you're the one making the argument and understanding their impact—that’s a different skill. 
Every logical fallacy on our list has a specific mechanism by which it damages B2B marketing programs. This guide covers the ones you're most likely to encounter in your day-to-day, what they look like in context, and how to call them out before they turn into a six-month unwind.

Mistaking correlation for causation is how marketing teams cut campaigns that were doing nothing wrong, and double down on tactics that were never responsible for the results they're claiming.

3. False cause: Your attribution model is lying to you
False cause is incorrectly identifying what caused something, typically by selecting the most convenient explanation while ignoring confounding variables.

The textbook example is ice cream sales correlating with drowning rates. Ice cream does not cause drowning. Both increase in summer. The season is the confounding variable neither metric accounts for. This is obvious in hindsight because it involves ice cream. But in marketing data, confounding variables are usually less visible and far more expensive.
The clearest institutionalized version of false cause in marketing is single-touch attribution. Last-click credits the final touchpoint before conversion and ignores everything that preceded it. First-click does the reverse. Both models assign causation to one variable while suppressing all others. 
The fix: Before claiming causation, ask: Is there a direct mechanism linking these two things? What confounds could explain both? Multi-touch attribution, incrementality testing, and media mix modeling exist specifically to address what single-touch models systematically hide. If you're still running last-click as your primary attribution model, you're not measuring what's working—you're confirming what converted last.

(Image Source)
Before claiming causation, ask: is there a plausible mechanism directly linking these two things? Are there confounding variables that could explain both? Has a controlled experiment ruled out the alternatives?
4. Narrative fallacy: The story you tell about your test results 
The narrative fallacy, named and popularized by Nassim Taleb in The Black Swan, is the tendency to construct a coherent causal story to explain a sequence of events, even when the events may be unrelated or the causation is unknown. The human mind is pattern-seeking by design. We are uncomfortable with randomness and tend to assign meaning to sequences whether or not meaning exists.

The narrative fallacy is what happens when your team gets better at explaining test results than at learning from them—constructing coherent causal stories for data that may be random, confounded, or simply unexplained.
Andrew Anderson, veteran CRO consultant and former Adobe optimization lead, used to run this exercise with new team members: 
Take any test you haven't seen the results for, and explain why every variant is clearly the best option and will obviously win. Initially they'd resist.  By the fourth or fifth test, they can do it effortlessly, constructing a persuasive narrative for any possible outcome.

(Image Source)
That's the problem. "Variant B won" is a fact. "Variant B won because red creates urgency and our users need urgency to convert" is a story you assembled after the result was known. It feels like insight but it's post-hoc rationalization.
As Anderson explains: 
"One of the greatest mistakes that people make is confusing the “why” that they generate in their head with the actual experience that they test. You can arrive at an experience in any number of ways and the story you tell yourself is just the mental model you used to create it. The experience is independent of that model yet people constantly are unable to dissociate them."
The fix: Don’t let a narrative govern the direction of the next experiment. Separate the result from the interpretation. Log what happened as a finding. Log your explanation as a hypothesis to be tested separately, not communicated as a conclusion. The narrative might be right. You don't know yet. 
5. Confirmation bias: How experimentation programs rot from the inside
Confirmation bias is the tendency to seek, interpret, and remember information that confirms existing beliefs while discounting information that contradicts them. 

Confirmation bias is technically a cognitive bias, but it belongs here because it's among the most consequential errors in reasoning for anyone running experiments.
Here's how it plays out: you hypothesize that adding a video to the landing page will lift conversion. You run a test. Results are inconclusive. You find methodological reasons to discount them—sample size, timing, segment composition—and run again. You continue until you have a result you can defend. The process looks rigorous, but the conclusion is compromised.
This isn't bad faith. It's the default mode of human cognition operating inside a system that was supposed to be immune to it.
The fix: Pre-registration. Before the experiment runs, commit in writing to your hypothesis, success metrics, minimum detectable effect, and decision criteria. Assigning someone to actively steelman the opposing hypothesis also helps. When results come in, you're evaluating against a pre-committed standard instead of a goalpost you can move. Assigning someone to actively argue the opposing hypothesis during design helps too. These don't eliminate the bias. They make it harder to act on.
6. Equivocation: The most underrated fallacy in marketing organizations
Equivocation uses a word with multiple meanings in different ways within the same argument, creating a misleading conclusion.

In business, equivocation often appears in performance reporting. Terms like "engagement," "reach," "conversion," and "qualified lead" can mean very different things to different people. When definitions shift mid-argument without acknowledgment, the conclusion does not follow from the premises.
A common scenario: sales and marketing are debating pipeline quality. Marketing is measuring "qualified leads" by ICP fit and engagement score. Sales is measuring "qualified leads" by whether the prospect has budget and authority confirmed. 
Both teams are using the same phrase to describe different populations. The debate is unresolvable, not because of a strategic disagreement, but because no one is discussing the same thing.
The fix: Define terms explicitly at the start of any cross-functional discussion involving data. If a key term appears more than once in an argument, verify it means the same thing each time. If you can't agree on a definition, you can't agree on a conclusion.
Fallacies that corrupt your strategic decisions
These errors don't distort how you read data. They distort how you choose direction: what to invest in, what to test, and which ideas survive the strategy process.
7. Appeal to authority: Why "Amazon does it this way" is not a strategy
An appeal to authority is when, in the face of conflicting evidence, you invoke the opinion of an expert to settle an argument. The problem isn’t referencing experts, but treating expert opinion as a replacement for evidence rather than one form of it.

In optimization, this fallacy appears in two ways. 
"Neil Patel said X matters most, so we should test X." Your website is not Neil Patel's. His audience, product, and context may be entirely different from yours. His heuristics are a starting point for forming hypotheses, not a substitute for testing them.
 "Amazon does it this way." Amazon operates at a scale, with a user base, and under competitive pressures that almost certainly do not apply to your product. Copying their decisions without understanding the evidence behind them is an appeal to authority.
Carl Sagan put it clearly: 
"One of the great commandments of science is, 'Mistrust arguments from authority.' Too many such arguments have proved too painfully wrong. Authorities must prove their contentions like everybody else."
The fix: Treat expert opinion as a hypothesis, not a conclusion. "Best practice says long-form landing pages convert better in this category" is a reason to test long-form. It's not a reason to ship it without testing on your audience.
8. Appeal to tradition: "We've always done it this way" is a description, not an argument
The appeal to tradition, also known as argumentum ad antiquitatem, is a fallacy where an idea is assumed to be correct because it has always been done that way. 

If you work in any organization that has been around for more than two years, you have heard this argument.
Many company cultures are genuinely resistant to change, which is the opposite of a culture of experimentation. The argument "we've always done it this way" is not evidence that the current approach is optimal. It is evidence that the current approach survived long enough to become default. That’s all.
Survival is not evidence of superiority over alternatives that were never tested. It's the same reasoning behind resistance to AI integration: "we've built our process around human review" is a description of history, not an argument against testing whether AI-assisted workflows produce better outcomes.
The fix: "We've always done it this way" is a prompt to ask why, not a reason to stop asking. The legitimate version of this argument is: "We've always done it this way, and here's the evidence it continues to outperform alternatives." Without that second clause, it's inertia, not strategy. 
Watch for the mirror error. Appeal to novelty—assuming something is better because it's new—is equally fallacious. In experimentation, this shows up as spaghetti testing: running experiments to introduce change rather than because evidence suggests a change will improve outcomes. Neither age nor newness is evidence of value.

9. False dilemma: A failed test is not a failed hypothesis
A false dilemma, also known as a false dichotomy, presents only two options when more exist. It’s frequently used as a rhetorical device to force a choice between an acceptable position and an unacceptable one, eliminating the actual range of alternatives.

In marketing, the most damaging version is what’s known as congruence bias: the tendency to test only the most obvious version of a hypothesis and treat a negative result as disconfirmation of the entire idea. 
"You create a false choice such as one banner versus another or one headline versus another, and then test to see which is better. In reality, there are hundreds of different ways that you can go, but we become blinded by our myopic view of an answer. It is the job of the researcher or tester to compare all valid alternative hypotheses, yet we get stuck on the things we want to see win."
— Andrew Anderson
Here's what it looks like: 
Research shows users are uncomfortable entering credit card information on your checkout page. So you test security badge vs. no security badge. The result shows no difference. You conclude the hypothesis was wrong.
But the hypothesis wasn't "a security badge will solve this." The hypothesis was "users feel unsafe at checkout." There are dozens of ways to address that: social proof, guarantee copy, simplified form design, payment option variety, trust-signal placement and hierarchy. You tested one execution and declared the idea dead. That's a false dilemma between this specific execution and nothing.
The fix: A failed test is a failed execution. Before calling a hypothesis dead, map how many executions haven't been tested yet. The idea may be right, but your implementation of it may have been wrong.

As Anderson advises: 
"Always make sure you avoid only testing what you think will win, and ensure that you are designing tests around what is feasible, not just the most popular opinion about how to solve the current problem."
10. Bandwagon fallacy: Competitive benchmarking is overrated
The bandwagon fallacy argues that something is correct or desirable because many people believe or do it. 

In marketing strategy, this is the intellectual foundation of most competitive benchmarking. "80% of companies in our category are investing in X" tells you about adoption rates. It tells you nothing about whether X is working for them, whether results justify the investment, or whether it would work for your audience and business model. Widespread adoption is data about behavior, not evidence of effectiveness.
Social proof is a legitimate psychological lever for converting your customers. It's not a valid input for your own strategic decisions. These are different uses of the same concept, and conflating them is expensive.
The fix: When competitive benchmarking drives a recommendation, separate adoption from effectiveness. What's the evidence X is producing measurable results, not just that competitors are running it? If you can't answer that, you're making a bandwagon argument with a market research label on it.

11. Sunk cost fallacy: Past spend is not a strategic argument

The sunk cost fallacy is continuing to invest in something because of past investment, even when the rational decision is to stop.

What this looks like in practice: An 18-month product build with poor adoption data continues because "we've come too far to quit." A channel with declining returns keeps budget because of the infrastructure and expertise built around it. 
The logic feels sound. It isn't. The 18 months and the infrastructure are already spent. They're gone regardless of what happens next. The only rational question is whether future investment generates sufficient return.
This is one of the most politically charged fallacies in organizations because stopping feels like admitting failure. In reality, it's recognizing that past decisions are irreversible and future decisions should be made on current evidence.
The fix: When evaluating whether to continue an investment, remove all past spend from the analysis. Ask only: Given what we know now, does future investment in this direction generate sufficient expected return? If yes, continue. If no, stop—regardless of what's been spent.
12. Circular reasoning: How brand investment justifies itself
Circular reasoning uses the conclusion as a premise in the argument intended to support it.

The brand marketing version is common: "We're the market leader because more people choose us than any competitor. More people choose us because we're the market leader." 
The two claims explain each other. Neither is independently supported. This is how significant brand spend gets defended—through self-referential logic that sounds like an argument but is a loop.
The fix: Ask what independently supports the claim, separate from the claim itself. If the only evidence for the conclusion is a restatement of the conclusion, the argument hasn't been made yet. Require a premise that can stand alone.
Fallacies that corrupt your organizational thinking
These errors don't primarily damage data analysis or strategic direction. They damage how teams discuss, challenge, and decide: the process by which ideas get tested, defended, or buried.
13. Ad hominem: How bad ideas survive internal challenges
Ad hominem means "to the person." Instead of addressing an argument, you attack the person making it. 

In formal debates this is obviously a deflection. In organizations, it's how weak strategies survive scrutiny. 
When someone challenges a campaign and the response is "you don't understand our audience"—that's direct ad hominem. This may be relevant context, but it does not make the analysis wrong. The data either supports the conclusion or it does not.
"Of course she thinks that, it's her team's initiative"—circumstantial. A conflict of interest is worth disclosing. But it doesn’t, by itself, invalidate the argument.
"Your last campaign missed targets too"—tu quoque. This deflects without responding. Even if true, it is not a counterargument.
None of these address whether the strategy is sound. All of them successfully move the conversation away from the actual question.
The test is simple: Could the same argument be made by a different person and still be valid? If yes, the response to the person is irrelevant to the argument's strength.
The fix: In reviews and discussions, respond to the argument on its merits. If a conflict of interest exists, name it as context and still address the substance. Winning the personal exchange is not the same as winning the argument.
If the data supports the conclusion, it doesn't matter who ran the analysis. Evaluate the argument, not the analyst.
14. Straw man: Arguing against a competitor that doesn't exist
A straw man argument misrepresents an opponent's position to make it easier to attack. You argue against a distorted, weaker version of what was actually said.

Internally: a team proposes moving from weekly to monthly reporting to reduce noise. The response: "So you want to fly blind for 30 days?" The proposal said nothing about removing monitoring. The critic built a weaker version of the argument and knocked that down.
In competitive messaging, this operates at the strategic level. Brands routinely write positioning against a straw version of a competitor's value proposition: one that's easier to dismiss than the actual case the competitor is making. 
This isn't just a logical error. It signals to sophisticated buyers that you haven't engaged seriously with the competitive landscape. They have.
The fix: When you suspect a straw man, restate the original argument explicitly: "That's not the claim—the claim was X. Do you have a response to that?" In competitive messaging, steelman the competitor's actual position before writing against it.
15. Appeal to ignorance: How zombie campaigns stay funded
An appeal to ignorance claims that something is true because it has not been proven false, or false because it has not been proven true.

The channel strategy version: "No study has shown this channel doesn't work for us, so we should keep running it." The absence of disconfirming evidence is not confirmation. This is how zombie campaigns survive—they haven't been proven ineffective, so they keep consuming budget while better-evidenced alternatives wait.
The burden of proof lies with the person making the positive claim. "You can't prove this doesn't work" is not a reason to fund it. It's a reason to measure it.
The fix: Require positive evidence for continued investment, not merely the absence of negative evidence. If a channel can't point to evidence it's working, the default should be reallocation, not continuation.
16. Slippery slope: The primary mechanism of testing paralysis
A slippery slope argument claims that a small first step will inevitably lead to extreme consequences, without evidence that the intermediate steps are likely.

In marketing organizations, this is how high-impact testing areas stay untouched. "If we test the checkout flow, we might break conversion and miss Q4 targets." "If we change the pricing page, sales will lose their anchor." Each step in that chain requires evidence. 
The catastrophic conclusion doesn't follow from the initial change—it's asserted rather than demonstrated. And the result is teams that run low-stakes tests indefinitely while leaving the decisions that actually move revenue untouched.
Note: The slippery slope isn't always a fallacy. If there's genuine evidence the intermediate steps are likely, the concern is legitimate. The fallacy is asserting the chain without that evidence.
The fix: When someone invokes a slippery slope to block testing, ask for the mechanism: "What's the specific path from testing X to outcome Z?" If they can't provide evidence for the intermediate steps, you're looking at an assumption dressed up as a risk assessment.
How to spot logical fallacies in real arguments
Four questions. Run them on any argument before accepting it.
Strip the delivery. Remove the confidence, the authority, and the emotional weight. Write out the actual claim and the premises offered in support. Does the conclusion follow? Arguments can feel compelling because of who's making them or how forcefully. Remove those and see if the structure holds.
Did it respond to what was actually said? Straw men, ad hominem, and appeals to ignorance all respond to something other than the original claim. When an argument seems to change the subject, return to the original question: Was it actually answered?
What alternatives were excluded? False dilemmas, hasty generalizations, and bandwagon arguments all depend on artificially narrowing the range of possibilities. Are these really the only options? Is this sample actually representative? Is adoption the same thing as effectiveness?
What evidence supports the causal claim? Post hoc reasoning, false cause, and narrative fallacy all assert causation without sufficient evidence. Is there a controlled experiment behind this? What confounds have been ruled out? Is correlation being reported as proof?
How to build arguments that don't collapse under scrutiny
State your premises explicitly. Fallacious arguments hide weak premises. Forcing yourself to articulate every assumption exposes exactly where the reasoning is thin. If a premise can't be stated clearly, it can't be defended.
Steelman the opposition. Before arguing against a position, build the strongest possible version of it. If you can't explain why a reasonable person might hold the opposing view, you don't understand the argument well enough to refute it, and your counterargument will show that.
Separate fact from interpretation. "Variant B increased conversion by 12%" is a fact. "Because users responded to the social proof element" is an interpretation. Keep these distinct in reports and in your own thinking. Label interpretations as hypotheses. Test them separately.
Pre-register your experiments. Commit to hypothesis, success metrics, minimum detectable effect, and decision criteria before the test runs. This is the structural fix for confirmation bias—it makes it significantly harder to move the goalposts once results are in.
Apply the burden of proof to yourself. "We should do X" requires a reason. "No one has proven we shouldn't" is not a reason. Hold your own claims to the same standard you'd apply to anyone else's.
Reasoning errors are common. Catching them is the competitive advantage.
The most expensive limitations in a marketing program rarely come from bad data or weak creative. They come from reasoning errors that feel like sound thinking. That's why they survive meetings, make it into strategies, and show up in budget allocations year after year.
The 16 fallacies here fall into three categories, each with a distinct cost:
Data interpretation fallacies: Hasty generalization, post hoc, false cause, narrative fallacy, confirmation bias, and equivocation produce incorrect conclusions from real data.
Strategic fallacies: Appeal to authority, appeal to tradition, false dilemma, bandwagon, sunk cost, and circular reasoning point you in the wrong direction.
Organizational fallacies: Ad hominem, straw man, appeal to ignorance, and slippery slope let bad ideas survive and kill good ones.
You'll recognize most of these in other people's arguments before you recognize them in your own. That's okay. The goal isn't perfect reasoning. It's building a habit of examining the structure of an argument, not just whether the conclusion sounds right.
But learning to identify them is step one. 
The value comes from applying that recognition in the moment: when a budget decision is being made on insufficient evidence, when a test is about to be stopped prematurely, or when a strategy is being defended because competitors are doing it.
