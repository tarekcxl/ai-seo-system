## The psychological associations of colors [Anchor link for: h the psychological associations of colors](https://cxl.com/blog/which-color-converts-the-best/\#h-the-psychological-associations-of-colors)

[The psychology of color](https://cxl.com/institute/online-course/psychology-neuroscience-cro/) plays a significant role in marketing, as different colors evoke specific emotions and associations that can influence consumer behavior.

Understanding these psychological associations helps brands shape how consumers feel and respond to their messaging.

### Orange

![orange color psychology](<Base64-Image-Removed>)

[(Image Source)](https://blog.hubspot.com/the-hustle/psychology-of-color)

Orange radiates confidence, creativity, and boldness, making it a great fit for fun, non-corporate brands. Its warmth is tied to the sun, giving it an energetic and inviting feel.

But it’s not all sunshine—orange can also evoke frustration, sluggishness, or even immaturity. No wonder 29% of people rank it as their least favorite color.

The use of orange CTA buttons, however, is popular because it evokes feelings of energy, excitement, and urgency, encouraging visitors to take action quickly.

![Mint Mobile's use of orange CTA buttons](https://lh7-rt.googleusercontent.com/docsz/AD_4nXf18ELFBg32TiDrpGoe2OLTIIRy3XNcOC6Q_H5KYR7VuLfc9Jgd6qfGgt3WU0x871mZGqnbNsvIwCrqGwMR-y5Xt7aTvcprtivFdQRhHTT7c6zBUyMajjKqyrSetr2Xmm3RQ7GG?key=zdmcwqj-qDoDfX2BoGks-foN)

[(Image Source)](https://www.mintmobile.com/)

Amazon, Ahrefs, and Mint Mobile are the perfect examples of brands leveraging its psychological and emotional effects. The color’s quirky, youthful energy and ability to create a sense of urgency and prompt users to take immediate action, make it the perfect color for “Buy Now” or “Get Started” buttons.

### Blue

![blue color psychology](<Base64-Image-Removed>)

[(Image Source)](https://blog.hubspot.com/the-hustle/psychology-of-color)

Blue is the world’s favorite color—57% of men and 35% of women rank it as their top choice. It’s also the most common logo color, used by 33% of top brands.

Why? Blue evokes security, strength, and trust. That’s why social media giants like Meta use it—it makes them seem dependable, which is crucial when handling user data.

But blue isn’t all positive. It can also feel cold or unwelcoming and is rare in natural foods. So, the fact that it can suppress appetite and is therefore rarely used in the food industry is not surprising.

![PayPal's homepage - use of blue CTA buttons](<Base64-Image-Removed>)

[(Image Source)](https://www.paypal.com/za/home)

Blue is commonly used by companies that want to establish trust, particularly for essential services. Think health insurance providers, financial organizations, IT businesses, home repairs, or pharmaceuticals, which is why brands like Facebook, PayPal, and Dropbox use it in their logos and CTAs. Their blue branding reinforces reliability, reassuring customers during important decisions.

### Cultural and geographical factors

Although the psychological impact of color is undeniable, it’s not a simple matter of one color always being better than another. The psychology is complex and often needs to be taken in context of cultural and geographical differences as they can have contradictory meanings.

For example, [white is the color of mourning and death in Chinese culture](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Color_in_Chinese_culture#White.2FSilver), but [the color of death in Brazil is purple](https://www.shutterstock.com/blog/color-symbolism-and-meanings-around-the-world). [Yellow symbolizes happiness and peace in Hinduism](https://www.timesnownews.com/spiritual/significance-of-colours-in-hinduism-article-107679746) but represents sadness in Greece and jealousy in France. [It’s also said that,](https://blog.hubspot.com/the-hustle/psychology-of-color) in North American culture, the color blue creates a feeling of trust, but also encourages appetite. Green supposedly means nature, freshness, growth, and money.

People from tropical countries respond favorably to warm colors; people from northern climates prefer cooler colors, and so on.

There’s _some_ truth to these interpretations, but can we translate these supposed associations into conversion lifts?

## Experiments with color usage: What the results have to say [Anchor link for: h experiments with color usage what the results have to say](https://cxl.com/blog/which-color-converts-the-best/\#h-experiments-with-color-usage-what-the-results-have-to-say)

[Studies](https://www.colorcom.com/research/why-color-matters) have shown that consumers decide whether they like a product within 90 seconds of viewing it, with up to 90% of that assessment based on color alone. These studies underscore the importance of color in shaping consumer perceptions of value, influencing their purchasing decisions and brand loyalty. Let’s take a look at various experiments and what they reveal about the effect of color in marketing.

### Impulse buying

Research indicates that red can encourage impulse purchases. A study cited by the [Daily Mail](https://www.dailymail.co.uk/femail/article-2870122/Infographic-reveals-tricks-stores-use-boost-sales-Christmas.html) found that the use of red in marketing materials led to more spontaneous buying decisions, whereas navy blue prompted more thoughtful and budget-conscious responses.

What’s more, a [study](https://academic.oup.com/jcr/article-abstract/39/5/947/1795180?redirectedFrom=fulltext&login=false) based on eBay data, revealed that red backgrounds increased the willingness-to-pay in negotiations and auctions in comparison to blue backgrounds.

**The proposition:** Bright, warm colors like red, orange, and yellow are particularly effective at creating a sense of urgency, excitement, and enthusiasm, which can encourage shoppers to make quick, unplanned purchases.

### Perception of value

Different colors evoke psychological associations that affect how people judge worth and pricing.  [Studies](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11747-010-0245-y) suggest that consumers tend to associate certain colors with specific price points.

For example, when the price of a product is displayed using warmer tones like red or orange, it can create a perception of urgency or affordability, making people believe they are getting a better deal.

On the other hand, cooler tones like blue and black or gold are often perceived as more premium or luxury, and this association influences their price perception and willingness to pay more.

And, when looking at backgrounds, studies found that prices displayed on light-colored backgrounds (like white or cream) were perceived as more expensive than those on dark-colored backgrounds (like black or navy blue), suggesting that color contrast plays a role in how consumers perceive the value of the price tag.

**The proposition:** Different colors influence how people perceive pricing and value, with warm tones creating a sense of urgency and affordability, while cool tones are linked to luxury and higher price points. Prices on light-colored backgrounds appear more expensive than those on dark backgrounds, highlighting the role of color contrast in price perception.

### Evoking specific emotions

An [analysis of 644 food and beverage companies](https://arxiv.org/abs/2407.16064?utm_source=chatgpt.com) revealed that red and gray were prevalent across all emotional categories, indicating their importance in branding. The study confirmed associations of yellow with happiness, blue with sadness, and bright colors with surprise, highlighting how color choices can shape consumer attitudes.

These insights underscore the complexity of using red in marketing. While it can effectively capture attention and drive certain behaviors, its impact varies based on context, cultural background, and the specific marketing objectives.

**The proposition:** Strategic color choices influence consumer perceptions. The use of red in marketing is complex—Red and gray are dominant across all emotional categories, while yellow with happiness, blue with sadness, and bright colors with surprise—highlighting the emotional impact of color in marketing.

### Brand recognition

[Research](https://straitsresearch.com/statistic/role-of-color-in-branding-and-marketing?utm_source=chatgpt.com) indicates that color can increase brand recognition by up to 80%. This heightened recognition stems from the emotional and psychological associations that colors invoke.

![color's role in branding](<Base64-Image-Removed>)

[(Image Source)](https://straitsresearch.com/statistic/role-of-color-in-branding-and-marketing?utm_source=chatgpt.com)

Maintaining consistent color usage across all branding materials enhances brand identity and recognition. Consistency helps consumers associate specific colors with a brand, reinforcing its presence in the market.

A [PMC study](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8005512/) underpinned the importance of consistency, revealing that if “the color of the sale price is inconsistent with that of the original price, consumers will form negative beliefs about the retailer. Furthermore, the color inconsistency of the original price and sale price will make consumers perceive that retailers are attempting to persuade them to buy, which will lead to less trust in the sale price.”

**The proposition:** Consistent color usage strengthens brand recognition and identity by creating strong psychological associations. Inconsistency in color, especially in pricing, can reduce consumer trust and negatively impact purchasing decisions.

### **Green vs. red: A call-to-action button war**

Over the years, there’s been controversy about which color converts best for your [CTA](https://cxl.com/blog/call-to-action-examples/) button. [**Unbounce**](https://unbounce.com/conversion-rate-optimization/the-future-of-marketing-call-to-action-buttons/) **declared that the future of CTA buttons was “BOB” (Big Orange Buttons).**

There are also a number of interesting [A/B testing](https://cxl.com/blog/ab-testing-guide//) case studies in which an orange or mostly red button was tested against other colors, often green.

When you think about it, green is mostly associated with positive emotions. When the traffic lights turn green, it means you can drive. Vice-versa with red. The color is mostly associated with negative emotions.

**So what do you think? Which button color won?** Here’s what the case studies had to say.

## **Why you should be skeptical of button color case studies** [Anchor link for: h why you should be skeptical of button color case studies](https://cxl.com/blog/which-color-converts-the-best/\#h-why-you-should-be-skeptical-of-button-color-case-studies)

Let’s take a look at one last example, which will help this make a whole lot more sense. [RIPT Apparel tested the color](https://vwo.com/blog/how-ript-apparel-increased-their-sales-by-6-3-ab-testing-case-study/) of their “Buy Now” button to see if it would make any difference to their bottom line.

This is the original version:

![ript original button color](<Base64-Image-Removed>)

[_Image credit_](https://vwo.com/blog/how-ript-apparel-increased-their-sales-by-6-3-ab-testing-case-study/)

And this was the new button:

![ript button color test](<Base64-Image-Removed>)

[_Image credit_](https://vwo.com/blog/how-ript-apparel-increased-their-sales-by-6-3-ab-testing-case-study/)

To no great surprise, conversion numbers went up. Looking at the previous cases, one could say that if they changed the button to red, it might convert even better. Well, not quite.

Take a look at the original again. Do you see something that _should_ be there but isn’t? The original is missing a button! The “Buy Now” CTA gets lost in the design. You can see the new button clearly, regardless of whether it’s green, red, yellow, or any of several colors.

This, unfortunately, is how the great controversy of button colors got started. You see amazing results that suggest that one color _always_ converts the best. That is, until you look closer.

More often than not, these tests reveal that, previously, there was no button, or the button is just much more prominent. **It stands out from the rest of the page and converts better because it’s a high-contrast color, not any single color.**

Monetate, which ran the blue vs. orange button test, had this to say:

_But, if you dig into the results, you’ll see that orange buttons were almost always tested against a control of no button at all. In cases like these, it’s hardly a surprise that orange buttons make a difference. Of course they do … when compared to no button at all! Practically any button will make a difference, regardless of color._

In the case of RIPT Apparel, they tested one final version, with a yellow button:

![yellow button](<Base64-Image-Removed>)

[_Image credit_](https://vwo.com/blog/how-ript-apparel-increased-their-sales-by-6-3-ab-testing-case-study/)

Conversions went up a further 6.3%. So yellow beats green? No, no, no.

### **The CTA button color makes little difference on its own**

**The color of the button has little-to-no effect on its own. What’s more important is how it changes the visual hierarchy of the page and how it makes the call-to-action stand out.**

It also depends on what we’re used to seeing. [Bing increased their revenue](http://shockmarketer.com/call-to-action-color/) by $80 million by finding the _exact_ color of blue for their links. People are used to seeing blue links. When the World Wide Web first came to be, blue was the color of links. (Microsoft engineers working on this also admitted that “…it was a shade of blue quite similar to the one used by Google.”)

**There is no best color for conversions.** In the yellow button’s case, they also added urgency (“$10 only for 24 hours”), which has been [demonstrated time and time again](https://cxl.com/research-study/urgency-ecommerce-product-page/) to lift conversions. In the case of Bing and blue, it was the power of convention in action.

### **Back to HubSpot**

The same can be said for the [HubSpot’s example.](https://blog.hubspot.com/blog/tabid/6307/bid/20566/The-Button-Color-A-B-Test-Red-Beats-Green.aspx) Take a look at it again:

![hubspot](<Base64-Image-Removed>)

[_Image credit_](https://blog.hubspot.com/blog/tabid/6307/bid/20566/The-Button-Color-A-B-Test-Red-Beats-Green.aspx)

Do you notice anything that might contribute to the red button being a better choice? Something in the overall design of the page?

Green is one of the main colors of the site! And what happens when you add a green button to the mix? It won’t stand out. They could have tested green against almost any other bright color and would have seen the same “amazing” results.

To HubSpot’s credit, they too say in the case study that they “cannot generalize these results to all situations.” But still, a green button on a page with a dominant green design? Really?

## Conclusion [Anchor link for: h conclusion](https://cxl.com/blog/which-color-converts-the-best/\#h-conclusion)

Color matters for your calls to action. But saying that one color converts better than another doesn’t hold. There is no universal best color. What works on one site doesn’t necessarily work on another.

Visual hierarchy matters. So does making your CTA buttons stand out with a high-contrast color. The “green vs. red” debate is less about color per se and more about whether the color helps the button stand out.
